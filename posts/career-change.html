Why I Changed Careers from Vet Med to Software Development

Three years ago, I accepted a job offer for a software developer position. At the time, I was working as a veterinarian in a small animal vet hospital. Quite the career pivot. I often get asked why I made such a drastic career change, especially after all the schooling/training involved in becoming a vet, and sometimes have trouble answering that question, despite knowing it was the right decision. This post is mostly an attempt to articulate the reason for this career change.


The Path to Veterinary Medicine

I, like many vets, became a veterinarian because I've loved animals ever since I was a kid. I started working at a local vet hospital in high school as an assistant. There I met a veterinary oncologist who also happened to work at the NIH doing comparative oncology research. Eventually, I landed myself a summer internship in his lab, assisting with his research. The idea of being able to help people or make breakthroughs in human medicine by studying animal models was intriguing, and as an introvert, I enjoyed working in the lab doing bench work.


During this time I was encouraged to pursue higher education by my mentor as well as my parents. This meant some type of graduate school either a PhD or PhD + vet school. In my last year of college I was offered a position in the lab at NIH as a postbac research fellow. I had also applied and was accepted into vet school. While I wasn't sure exactly what type of career I wanted to pursue, working on cutting edge cancer research was certainly an intriguing opportunity. At the end of the day, pressure from family led me to accept the veterinary school offer, afraid I'd never go back to school and become a vet later if I didn't go then (in hindsight, that probably wouldn't have been such a bad thing).


I graduated from veterinary school in 2017 and went on to complete two years of additional training (internships) thinking I wanted to pursue a residency in oncology, then a PhD, following in the footsteps of my mentor from the NIH. Pretty quickly, I realized veterinary medicine as a whole was just not for me. I abandoned the idea of a residency and started working as a general practice vet. I also started learning to code and began the long journey of changing careers and leaving behind full-time work as a vet, after 6 long years of vet school and internships. So the question is, why?


The Human Factor

I'm often asked by summer students or assistants if I would recommend becoming a veterinarian. I usually echo what I've heard other vets say to similar questions: "if there's literally anything else you can imagine yourself doing, you should pursue that instead, otherwise, maybe you should become a vet". I also ask them if the idea of talking to people for 10 hours a day sounds appealing. If not, it's probably not the right profession to get into. Of course, veterinarians interact with animals every day too: physical exams, procedures, and surgery are part of the job; but those tasks didn't fulfill my love for animals the way people often assume they would. If anything, it made me like animals less than I used to; or at least be more weary of them. Veterinary medicine, in my opinion, is about 90% working with and managing people and 10% working with animals (who are occasionally trying to bite and/or scratch your face off).

Communication and education occur constantly with pet owners, understandably so, as our patients can't communicate or advocate for themselves. This means when you're seeing 20-25 appointments every day, you have 20-25 conversations with different people. These conversations can often be mentally and/or emotionally taxing: limited finances, optimal treatment options (for both the pet and the owner), discussion of prognosis, euthanasia.. Managing expectations can also be difficult: it's possible we might not find an answer even after running costly diagnostics, leading to increased frustration for pet owners. At the same time, we must manage any hospitalized patients, field phone calls, and delegate staff. At the end of most days, I found my 'social battery' to be running on empty.


Lack of Personal Connection

Despite deeply caring about every patient I see and working with owners towards optimal outcomes, I struggled to find passion within the profession. Over the past nearly decade of practicing, I've found there to be a big difference between loving animals and being passionate about veterinary medicine. In light of that, I've accepted that it's something that can't be forced. I will of course always like animals; but I don't think I'll ever find passion in veterinary medicine as a profession.


The Operational Challenges

Throughout my time working as a veterinarian, whether full-time, part-time or relief, in corporate practices or private practice, staffing issues have been a constant problem. Being a veterinary technician is hard work, and unfortunately, unlike in human medicine where being a nurse requires a degree and returns a good salary, being a veterinary technician nets on average roughly $20-30/hr [1].
It's no surprise then that long hours, physical and emotional stress, and low pay (among other things) frequently lead to burnout among veterinary technicians and assistants. This leads to frequent turnover, which results in many untrained or newly trained technicians and assistants. One of the results of this cycle is that both veterinarians and experienced technicians are then left to compensate for others' gaps in knowledge/reliability (leading to more stress, long hours, etc...).


Personal Misalignment

Overall, I found my ideal work environment involves deep technical collaboration rather than continuous client-facing interactions. I excel in discussions with developers, stakeholders, and managers to solve technical problems, communicate requirements, and translate those requirements into robust systems. Work that allows for both focused problem solving and meaningful, focused techinical dialogue. I discovered (albeit 20 years late) that I could actually make a career out of my lifelong fascination with computers and technology. I found joy in creating and building things that people will use and solving complex problems by writing code and building software. Perhaps most importantly, I've found that when I encounter a difficult problem in software development, instead of frustration, I'm genuinely excited. It's an opportunity to learn, to improve, to build something better. I found passion in this new career.


While changing careers wasn't easy, I have no regrets about the path that led me here. Had I not become a veterinarian, I wouldn't have met my wife when we both worked at the same hospital. My time in veterinary medicine also taught me about communication, working under pressure, and complex problem solving, valuable skills in my current career and in life in general. I dont discourage anyone from becoming a veterinarian. Rather, I'd encourage anyone who's unsure of their career to consider what truly energizes you and to pursue work that aligns with who you really are.
