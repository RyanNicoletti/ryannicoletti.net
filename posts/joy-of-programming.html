Finding Joy in Programming in the Age of LLM's

I love writing code. I enjoy the actual act of typing the syntax of different programming languages into a text editor. I enjoy the feeling and sound of the keyboard, googling syntax questions, searching for the answer to bugs, and debugging a complicated program. The feeling of solving a complicated coding problem after hours, sometimes days, of searching for an answer is unmatched. Of course, all of these hours spent spelunking the web for answers aren't wasted. I almost always end up with a greater understanding of the underlying technology/language/framework that I'm researching. And when I "finish" a project (are personal projects ever really finished?), I get the absolute pleasure of actually seeing and interacting with the results of my hard work. All of these things bring me much joy.



I have been thinking a lot about how much I love coding, and how lucky I am to be able to do this for a living. Unfortunately, as agents and agentic coding become more commonplace, coding without the assistance of an LLM seems to be coming to an end. I'm certainly not anti-AI and I use claude everyday to code (LLM's are amazing technology!). When I use an LLM to write code for/with me, I've found the experience to be a bit less enjoyable compared to the "old" way of writing code pre-LLM's. I don't get that "AHA!" moment if I use the LLM to debug for me or when reviewing the generated code. I don't get the dopamine hit that comes from thinking deeply about difficult problems and finally figuring a solution to some bug. I don't think I gain as much knowledge/understanding of the code as I would have from reading through docs, blog posts, or stack overflow comments from other developers that are smarter than me who have encountered these problems already. The plus side of this is that AI enables me to build things much faster, which is awesome!



With that said, I'm interested to see what happens in the future as there seems to be increasing pressure for developers to use LLM's and agents to generate all/most of their code. Even if this is not strictly enforced (although in some companies, it already is ), there is the concern that those that don't use LLM's might be viewed as less productive than the LLM users.



It's clear that for better or for worse, generative AI is here to stay. While it's a bit sad to me that the ultimate result of LLM's in software development is the "death" of crafting good, maintainable, quality code by hand; I'm also excited to see what the future brings and what new software people will be capable of building with these incredible tools. For the time being, when working on personal projects, I tend to minimize my LLM usage. Especially when learning a new language/technology, I find this gives me a deeper understanding of things. Then, once I am more proficient on my own, I find I'm able to move faster and with more confidence when equipped with an LLM. I'm hopeful that whatever the future brings, I will continue to learn about computers and experience the joy of programming.
